\begin{abstract}

Delays in air travel can lead to significant confusion for the passenger and create operational disruption for the airline. The purpose of this work is to investigate the delay of airline flights by analyzing historical airline flight data, using two types of machine learning techniques, classification (to predict if the flight will be delayed) and regression (to estimate the delay time). The training datasets used for both types of models are publicly accessible. Model comparison metrics used include accuracy, precision, recall, and mean square error.  

In addition to model performance being important, this work will demonstrate the practicality of these types of models by providing an interactive interface (with Streamlit) to allow users to enter their own flight information into the system to receive real-time predictions and visual insights. Multi-variable models are expected to outperform their simple baseline counterparts but will also produce a better balance between the two aspects: i.e., for automated predicting, and for providing a better understanding of how the model works. Overall, this work demonstrates a use of machine learning to solve real-world transportation problems, while still being user-friendly and easily accessible to all users.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Motivation: 
The problem of flight delays in the U.S. continues to exist. Flight delays impact many passengers every year and negatively impact them in many different ways, including causing passengers to miss connecting flights, causing higher costs, making it harder to achieve reliable travel, etc. Currently, there are very few tools that provide predictive information on flight delays; most only provide a historical record of what the airline has done previously or update the passenger with real-time information. The other issue is that passengers generally are not familiar with why certain flights are delayed and rarely know what to do to obtain a better travel option. In light of this issue, we propose an application using machine learning to allow the passenger to enter their departing airport, arriving airport, and date of travel and receive a prediction of both the probability of flight delay and the predicted duration of the flight delay based on the individual airline being used for the flight. In addition, users would also receive an airline recommendation based on the risk of flying with that airline so that they can make better travel choices.

Technical focus: We define this problem as a classification task (predicting whether a flight will be delayed) and a regression task (predicting the duration of the delay). To capture the complex relationships among numerous factors such as weather, airline operations, and airport congestion, we employed a combination of baseline models and ensemble models, including logistic regression and forest models. One of the key contributions of our approach lies in the use of five structured delay categories from the BTS dataset - airline, weather, national aviation system, security check, and delayed aircraft delay - as core features. This not only enables us to predict the delay situation but also explains the underlying causes and provides users with more meaningful suggestions.

Prior Work: Delay prediction through previous research has mainly used machine learning or statistical analysis to analyse numerous delays based upon past events to develop delay patterns. This research attempts to find the most significant factors contributing to delays as well as improve delay prediction accuracy. However, almost all other delay prediction research used single outcome variables for delay prediction purposes and did not define a method to classify the cause of each delay) and did not provide an avenue for extending research efforts into user-friendly applications. Therefore, our research will be different in that we define multiple delay type classifications, and bring together delay prediction with a user-friendly decision-support system to aid in decision making.

Machine Learning Pipeline: Our pipeline includes data collection, exploratory data analysis (EDA), preprocessing, model training, model evaluation, and deployment. We worked with 2025 Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) flight data to get information on the airline and route, and delay features to include in our models. The delay features we used included features from carrier delays, weather delays, NAS delays, security delays, and late aircraft delays. We also used EDA to examine the delay distributions for routes, airlines, and regions to find patterns related to the delays for each type. After identifying the missing values in the data and encoding the categorical variables, we built classification and regression algorithms to predict the likelihood of or length of a delay, with the training/testing split giving us an accurate assessment of the models. The performance of the model was evaluated with accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score, and RMSE. Once the best-performing model was identified, it was deployed through a Streamlit application that allows the user to input the desired route and date, and to subsequently receive the likelihood of the delay occurring, which is based on the route and date the user provided, along with any other likely contributing factors to the delay.

Impact: The objective of this study is to assist users in making flight travel decisions by providing them with predictive analysis, enabling them to gain a deeper understanding of flight delays. This will help users better identify the causes of delays, allowing them to choose more reliable travel options and optimize their travel plans. Although this study will provide users with insights into the causes of delays in specific regions, due to the large number of large airports, data bias may affect the accuracy of the model, and thus the results may not be applicable to the entire range of airport codes. Therefore, we will provide users with probability prediction estimates and simultaneously show them the uncertainty and its influencing factors.

\section{Background}

Flight delay prediction has been widely studied using both statistical and machine learning methods. Early work commonly used regression and classification models to predict delays based on historical flight data \cite{tang2021airline}. More recent studies have improved prediction performance by applying advanced methods, such as ensemble models and neural networks, to capture more complex relationships in the data \cite{jiang2020aviation}. Prior research has also shown that flight delays are influenced by multiple factors, including airline operations, departure time, and weather conditions \cite{choi2016weather}.

Recent literature further suggests that flight delays are not isolated events. Instead, delays can propagate across flights and through airport networks, which makes prediction more challenging \cite{chen2019chained, wu2019propagation}. Although these approaches often improve predictive accuracy, many of them rely on more complex models that are harder to interpret and apply in practice \cite{zoutendijk2021probabilistic}.

Our proposed approach is similar to prior work in that it also uses historical flight data and machine learning to predict delays. However, our work differs from prior studies in two main ways. First, we focus on directly comparing both classification and regression methods within a simpler and more interpretable framework. Second, we emphasize usability by integrating the models into an interactive Streamlit application. Based on findings from prior work, we expect that our multi-variable models will outperform simple baselines, while offering a better balance between performance, interpretability, and ease of use than more complex approaches.

\subsubsection{Data Collection, Exploration \& Processing} 

We use the 2025 BTS Airline On-Time Performance dataset obtained from the official U.S. Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) TranStats Airline On-Time Performance Reporting download portal. This is an official U.S. government data source that provides public flight performance records, making it an appropriate and reliable dataset for our project. After merging the 12 monthly files for 2025, the dataset contains 6,390,044 rows and 23 columns, which provides broad coverage across airlines, origins, destinations, and dates. It is a structured tabular dataset with mixed data types. It includes temporal features (YEAR, QUARTER, MONTH, FL\_DATE), categorical features (OP\_UNIQUE\_CARRIER, ORIGIN, ORIGIN\_CITY\_NAME, ORIGIN\_STATE\_NM, DEST, DEST\_CITY\_NAME, DEST\_STATE\_NM), numerical features (DEP\_DELAY, DEP\_DELAY\_NEW, AIR\_TIME, DISTANCE, CARRIER\_DELAY, WEATHER\_DELAY, NAS\_DELAY, SECURITY\_DELAY, LATE\_AIRCRAFT\_DELAY, etc.), and a binary target-related variable (DEP\_DEL15). In total, the merged dataset has 11 float columns, 4 integer columns, and 8 object columns. We will use this dataset to build a model to investigate whether it is possible to predict flight delays in the United States based on weather, airline, departure or arrival cities, and operational factors. We will also use this to create a system that helps users choose more reliable airlines when traveling. The dataset already includes ground truth labels. We will use DEP\_DEL15 as the classification label and DEP\_DELAY\_NEW as the regression label.

We conducted an analysis of the types and causes of delays across various airlines. First, we analyzed the cleaned dataset, which contains only flights with complete data, and calculated the average forward departure delay time (DEP\_DELAY\_NEW) and the percentage of flights delayed by 15 minutes or more (DEP\_DEL15) for each airline. As shown in Figure 1, our calculations reveal significant differences among airlines. The average forward departure delay ranges from approximately 44.82 to 85.48 minutes, with OH, F9, G4, AA, and OO exhibiting higher values, whereas AS, HA, MQ, and WN show relatively lower values. Meanwhile, the proportion of flights delayed by 15 minutes or more ranges from approximately 69.92 percentage to 85.97 percentage, with WN, G4, OH, NK, and AA having higher rates, while AS, YX, UA, and HA have relatively lower rates. Based on these statistics, we have created a bar chart showing the average delay time per flight by airline (Figure 2) and a bar chart showing the proportion of delayed flights by airline (Figure 3).

During the preprocessing stage, we first merged the 12 monthly CSV files into a single dataset and examined the overall structure, column types, missing values, and descriptive statistics. We then removed rows with missing values from the key delay-related columns and performed data cleaning. Specifically, if any of the variables DEP\_DELAY, DEP\_DELAY\_NEW, DEP\_DEL15, CARRIER\_DELAY, WEATHER\_DELAY, NAS\_DELAY, SECURITY\_DELAY, or LATE\_AIRCRAFT\_DELAY contained NAN values, we excluded the entire column of data. This is because the primary research question of our project is: Can we use weather, airline, departure/arrival cities, and operational factors to predict whether U.S. flights will be delayed? Therefore, we only need to retain data related to these factors and do not need to retain information on flights that departed on time or ahead of schedule. Since our delay data contains negative values (early departures), we truncated these negative values to 0 to ensure that the delay measurements are valid non-negative values. We also created variables for each airline, including delay rate, average delay time per flight, total delay time, and the percentage of delayed flights, to understand the relationship between airlines and delays. This provides valuable insights for the subsequent research in our project. Moreover, we also categorized the continuous variable “DISTANCE” into short-haul and long-haul flights. Flights with a distance of 500 miles or less were classified as short-haul, while those with a distance greater than 1,500 miles were classified as long-haul.

\subsection{Front-End (Streamlit)}

The front-end of the proposed web application is developed using the Streamlit framework, which enables rapid integration of machine learning models with an interactive and user-friendly interface. The goal of the application is to translate predictive modeling results into actionable insights that assist users in making better travel decisions.

\subsubsection{Target Population}

The primary target users of this application are travelers within the United States, including business travelers, students, and tourists, who seek to reduce the risk of flight delays. These users typically do not have a technical background in machine learning; therefore, the application is designed to be intuitive, requiring minimal input while providing clear and interpretable outputs.

\subsubsection{User Interface Layout}

The web application is structured into two main sections: an input interface and a results dashboard.

The input interface (home page) allows users to specify flight details, including departure airport, arrival airport, and travel date. The design follows a clean and minimal layout to reduce cognitive load and ensure ease of use.

\subsubsection{User Inputs and Outputs}

The application utilizes simple and structured input components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Dropdown menus for selecting departure and arrival airports
    \item A date picker for selecting the travel date
    \item A button labeled \textit{``Predict \& Compare Flights''} to trigger model execution
\end{itemize}

After the user submits the inputs, the application generates the following outputs:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Predicted probability of flight delay (classification output)
    \item Predicted delay duration in minutes (regression output)
    \item A ranked comparison of airlines based on delay risk
    \item A recommended airline based on combined prediction metrics
    \item An explanation of delay causes using feature importance

These outputs are presented visually and textually to enhance interpretability and usability.

\subsubsection{Front-End and Back-End Integration}

The Streamlit application integrates the front-end interface with the machine learning pipeline in a seamless manner. When the user clicks the prediction button, the selected inputs are passed to the backend model pipeline.

The backend consists of both a baseline Logistic Regression model and a primary Random Forest model. The Random Forest model is used to generate the final predictions, including delay probability and expected delay duration. Additionally, feature importance scores derived from the model are used to explain the contributing factors behind flight delays.

Once the model inference is complete, Streamlit dynamically updates the interface to display the results in the form of tables, charts, and recommendation panels, as shown in Figures~\ref{fig:frontend_delay_chart} and~\ref{fig:frontend_delay_causes}. \section{Risk \& Mitigation}

The disparity in categories of flight delays presents a substantial barrier to establishing a model of flight delays due to the unequal distribution (approximately half of delays are concentrated into two categories). Additionally, larger airports are likely to provide much more flight delay data than smaller ones would, thereby causing bias and skewness in the results produced by the flight delay model being developed. In order to alleviate the effects of such bias and skewness in regard to the model developed from the data provided by airports of varying size, we carry out an exploratory data analysis, give consideration to normalizing the features associated with the delay data, and evaluate the model’s performance by way of an assessment of performance over various routes and types of airports. Modelling the complex, nonlinear interactions between factors that produce flight delays is another challenge; factors creating delay include, for example, weather conditions and operational constraints. We have employed both baseline and ensemble type models with the hope of more thoroughly capturing the interaction among the various factors affecting delay, and we have compared the performance of the two types of models based upon multiple evaluation metrics in order to promote stability in our model results.


\section{Expected Outcomes }

Based on previous research findings and the characteristics of the BTS data set, ensemble models are expected to provide better predictions of the probability and duration of delays than baseline models. In addition, based on the analysis performed to date, it is anticipated that there will be only a few key factors contributing to overall delay patterns, these factors are likely to include late aircraft delays and carrier delays while climate-based delays may fluctuate more by geographic location and season. Moreover, the results of prediction analyses and delay factor analyses should provide valuable airline selection recommendations to users through the application's ability to identify the least risky travel options. Overall, this project seeks to demonstrate that integrating predictive modelling with interpretable delay factors improves the accuracy of the predictions, as well as the usefulness of the resulting decision support.
